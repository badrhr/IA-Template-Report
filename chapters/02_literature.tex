\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:lit_rev}

% [FIX 1] Added introductory paragraph — was completely missing in original.
This chapter reviews the existing literature relevant to the investigated problem. 
It situates the project within the current state of the art, identifies gaps in 
existing knowledge, and demonstrates the significance and motivation of this work. 
The review is organised as follows: Section~\ref{sec:lit_organisation} provides 
guidance on structuring a literature review; Section~\ref{sec:lit_synthesis} 
discusses critical analysis and synthesis; Section~\ref{sec:lit_citation} covers 
referencing and citation conventions; and Section~\ref{sec:lit_plagiarism} 
addresses plagiarism avoidance.

\noindent A literature review chapter might contain the following:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A review of the state-of-the-art (include theories and solutions) of 
    the field of research.
    \item A description of the project in the context of existing literature 
    and products/systems.
    \item An analysis of how the review is relevant to the intended 
    application/system/problem.
    \item A critique of existing work compared with the intended work.
\end{enumerate}
Note that your literature review should demonstrate the significance of the project.

\section{Organising Your Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit_organisation}
% ================================================================

A literature review is not a list of paper summaries. It must be organised 
around ideas, themes, or arguments — not around individual papers. There are 
two main organisational strategies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Thematic organisation (recommended):} Group sources by topic, 
    concept, or research theme. For example, one section may review methods for 
    feature extraction, another for classification, and another for evaluation 
    benchmarks. This approach shows the reader the landscape of ideas and how 
    they relate to each other.

    \item \textbf{Chronological organisation:} Arrange sources from earliest to 
    most recent to show how the field has evolved over time. This is useful when 
    the development of a concept over time is central to your argument.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Regardless of the structure chosen, each section of your literature 
review should:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Open with a sentence that states what the section is about.
    \item Discuss multiple sources together and compare them, rather than 
    describing each paper in isolation.
    \item End with a sentence that connects the reviewed material to your 
    own project.
\end{itemize}

\section{Synthesis and Critical Analysis}
\label{sec:lit_synthesis}
% ================================================================

The most important skill in a literature review is synthesis: combining 
information from multiple sources to build a coherent argument, rather than 
simply summarising each paper in turn. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Summarising} (avoid as a primary mode): ``Smith (2020) proposed 
    method X. Jones (2021) proposed method Y. Lee (2022) proposed method Z.''

    \item \textbf{Synthesising} (aim for this): ``Several methods have been 
    proposed for X, including approach A \citep{lamport1994latex}, approach B 
    \citep{kottwitzlatex2021}, and approach C. While A and B share the assumption 
    that..., C differs by... However, none of these approaches address the problem 
    of..., which is the gap this project targets.''
\end{itemize}

\noindent Critical analysis means evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of 
existing work — not just reporting what was done. For each group of sources, ask:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What are the key assumptions made, and are they justified?
    \item What are the limitations of the proposed methods?
    \item How do the results compare across different studies?
    \item What remains unresolved or contested in the literature?
\end{itemize}

\section{Citation and Referencing Guide}
\label{sec:lit_citation}
% ================================================================

The references in a report relate your content with the relevant sources, papers, 
and the works of others. To include references in a report, we \textit{cite} them 
in the text. In \LaTeX, the most convenient way is to use BibTeX, which takes 
references in a particular format [see \texttt{references.bib} file of this 
template] and formats them in the required style (APA, Harvard, IEEE, etc.) 
with the help of appropriate packages.

Take note of the commands \textbackslash cite\{\} and \textbackslash citep\{\}. 
The command \textbackslash cite\{\} produces ``Author et al. (2019)'' style for 
Harvard/APA. The command \textbackslash citep\{\} produces ``(Author et al., 2019).'' 
Depending on how you construct a sentence, use them appropriately. 
This Department recommends the \textbf{Harvard style} of referencing.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \cite{kottwitzlatex2021} has written a comprehensive guide on writing 
    in \LaTeX~[Example of \textbackslash cite\{\}].
    \item If \LaTeX~is used efficiently and effectively, it helps in writing a 
    very high-quality project report~\citep{lamport1994latex}~[Example of 
    \textbackslash citep\{\}].
    \item A detailed APA, Harvard, and Chicago referencing style guide are 
    available in~\citep{uor_refernce_style}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Changing Bibliography Styles}
While this report uses name-and-date formatting in the Harvard style, you may 
also wish to use a numbered style such as IEEE. To enable this, edit the 
\path{CS_report.sty} file. Uncomment the 2 lines of Harvard settings under 
\texttt{Bibliography/References settings}, and enable the IEEE style:

\begin{verbatim}
% IEEE, Numbered Style
\usepackage[numbers]{natbib}
\bibliographystyle{IEEEtran}
\end{verbatim}

Note that when making this change, you will need to modify the way in which 
you refer to authors by name using \verb|\citeauthor{}|.
