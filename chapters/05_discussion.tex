\section{Interpretation of Results}
\label{sec:interpretation}
% ================================================================

The Discussion and Analysis chapter evaluates and interprets the results 
presented in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}. Unlike the Results chapter, which reports
findings objectively, this chapter explains \textit{what the results mean}, 
\textit{why they occurred}, and \textit{how they relate to the research 
objectives and existing literature}.

When interpreting your results, consider the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Relate back to objectives:} For each result, explicitly state
    whether it supports, partially supports, or contradicts the corresponding
    research objective or hypothesis.

    \item \textbf{Compare with the literature:} Situate your findings within the
    broader body of knowledge. Do your results agree with or diverge from prior
    work? If they diverge, propose a reasoned explanation.

    \item \textbf{Explain unexpected outcomes:} If any result was surprising or
    inconsistent with expectations, discuss possible causes such as data quality,
    model assumptions, or experimental constraints.

    \item \textbf{Avoid repetition:} Do not simply restate the numbers from the
    Results chapter. Instead, provide analytical commentary that adds new insight.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation for Algorithm-Based Projects}
\label{subsec:algo-interpretation}

If your project involves algorithm implementation or evaluation, interpret the
measured performance in context. For example:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Does the achieved accuracy meet the threshold required for the
    application domain?
    \item How does the proposed algorithm compare to the baseline in terms of
    performance, complexity, and scalability?
    \item Are the gains in accuracy justified given any trade-offs in computational
    cost or memory usage?
    \item Do the results generalise across different configurations or datasets,
    or are they specific to the experimental conditions?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation for Data-Driven Projects}
\label{subsec:data-interpretation}

If your project addresses research questions over a collected dataset, interpret
each finding in relation to its corresponding research question:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Does the finding provide a clear and direct answer to the research
    question, or is the evidence only partial?
    \item Are there patterns, correlations, or trends that were not anticipated
    in the problem formulation?
    \item How do the findings contribute to the understanding of the investigated
    problem beyond what was already known from the literature?
\end{itemize}

\section{Significance of the Findings}
\label{sec:significance}
% ================================================================

In this section, discuss the significance of your results and key findings in
order to enhance the reader's understanding of the investigated problem.
Significance can be assessed at multiple levels:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Technical significance:} Does the work introduce a novel method,
    improve an existing approach, or demonstrate a result that advances the
    technical state of the art? For example, a meaningful improvement in
    classification accuracy or a reduction in computational complexity may
    constitute technical significance.

    \item \textbf{Practical significance:} Can the findings be applied in a
    real-world context? Discuss whether the results are actionable, deployable,
    or useful for practitioners in the relevant domain.

    \item \textbf{Scientific significance:} Do the findings confirm, challenge,
    or extend existing theories or models in the field? Even negative results
    can be scientifically significant if they rule out an assumed relationship
    or expose a limitation in a widely used method.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Where applicable, explicitly state how the findings address the gap in
the literature identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:lit_rev}, and what new knowledge
this project contributes.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ================================================================

Every project operates under constraints that may affect the validity,
generalisability, or completeness of its findings. Acknowledging limitations
transparently demonstrates critical thinking and scientific maturity. Discuss
the key limitations of your project under the following aspects:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data limitations:} Were there constraints on the size,
    diversity, or quality of the dataset? For instance, a small or imbalanced
    dataset may limit the generalisability of the results to broader populations
    or real-world conditions.

    \item \textbf{Methodological limitations:} Are there assumptions underlying
    your chosen methods that may not hold in all cases? For example, certain
    algorithms assume linearity, stationarity, or independence of features.

    \item \textbf{Scope limitations:} Were certain aspects of the problem left
    unexplored due to time, resource, or access constraints? Be explicit about
    what is outside the scope of this work and why.

    \item \textbf{Evaluation limitations:} Are the chosen evaluation metrics
    fully representative of real-world performance? For instance, accuracy alone
    may be misleading in the presence of class imbalance.

    \item \textbf{Reproducibility:} Are there factors in your experimental setup
    (e.g., randomness, proprietary tools, hardware dependency) that may affect
    the reproducibility of the results?
\end{itemize}

\noindent Where relevant, suggest potential improvements or directions that could
address these limitations in future work. This naturally transitions into the
recommendations discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:con}.
